1. THE QUESTION
Strip away the AI-supercycle slogans, the energy-transition liturgies, and the breathless headlines about $15,000/t copper. The question facing a portfolio manager today is colder and more specific: at LME three-month copper trading near $13,000/t (≈$5.90/lb on COMEX) on April 30, 2026 — a price that already prints a 50%+ year-on-year gain — does copper still offer asymmetric reward to capital deployed on a 2-to-5 year horizon, USD-denominated, in physical and equity expressions of the industrial play?
The answer this paper defends is yes, but with a discipline the consensus narrative refuses to apply. The bull case is not "electrification." The bull case is that mined supply cannot mathematically meet demand growth that is locked in by infrastructure already under construction, by capital spending decisions already approved, and by geopolitical realignments already legislated. The bear case requires a conjunction of low-probability events — synchronized recession, sustained substitution, supply-side miracles, and a recycling surprise — that taken together approach the boundary of the credible. The paper that follows is not a recommendation to buy every dip blindly. It is an argument that the structural balance of risk, after the April 2, 2026 tariff restructuring and the El Teniente, Grasberg, and Kamoa disruptions, has shifted further toward the long side than consensus pricing reflects. We will quantify that. We will name the invalidators. We will refuse to hedge with adverbs.
2. SUPPLY
The supply picture is not deteriorating gradually. It is deteriorating in chunks, and 2025 was the year the chunks became visible to anyone bothering to read the disclosures.
Codelco, the world's largest copper producer, suffered the deadliest mining accident in decades on July 31, 2025, when a seismic rockburst at El Teniente killed six workers in the Andesita section. Production at the world's largest underground copper mine, which had run at 356,000 tonnes in 2024, has been told by the company itself to expect roughly 300,000 tonnes annually through to the end of the decade. That is 56,000 tonnes per year of permanent capacity loss at a single asset, plus an internal audit subsequently uncovered "inconsistencies and concealment" in technical reports submitted around an earlier 2023 rockburst, prompting executive dismissals, criminal complaints, and the seizure of devices from former managers. Codelco is not just losing tonnes; it is losing institutional credibility at the precise moment the incoming Kast administration in Chile has signaled an audit of its finances and operations. In Indonesia, Freeport-McMoRan's Grasberg mine — the second-largest copper mine on the planet — declared force majeure following a fatal mudslide on September 8, 2025, that buried 800,000 wet tonnes of material across multiple underground levels. Benchmark Mineral Intelligence estimates cumulative supply losses from Grasberg between September 2025 and end-2026 at nearly 600,000 tonnes — roughly the annual output of Collahuasi, the world's seventh-largest mine. 3. DEMAND
The demand side has fragmented into three distinct vectors, two of which are accelerating in ways that make the third — the much-discussed Chinese property drag — increasingly irrelevant to the aggregate balance.
The first vector is the electrification stack. Grid investment, data centers, and electric vehicles are not "narratives." They are line items in capital budgets that have been signed, allocated, and broken ground. Goldman Sachs estimates AI data center buildout alone will require an additional 330,000–420,000 tonnes of copper annually by 2030, with each gigawatt of hyperscale capacity consuming 50,000–65,000 tonnes for high-voltage cabling, power distribution, and cooling. The OpenAI Stargate program, at $500 billion of announced spend, has already secured copper supply contracts. Each EV consumes three to four times the copper of an internal-combustion equivalent; with global EV sales at five-year highs and ICSG projecting 4.3 million tonnes of cumulative EV-driven demand by 2035, this is no longer a forecast — it is a backlog. FinancialContent + 3
The second vector — and this is where the original paper got China wrong by treating it as a monolith — is grid build-out, which is decoupled from the residential property cycle. State Grid Corporation of China invested approximately $89 billion (650 billion yuan) in 2025, up from roughly $80 billion in 2024. In January 2026, State Grid announced a 4 trillion yuan (≈$574 billion) commitment for 2026–2030 under the 14th Five-Year Plan period — a 40% increase over the prior plan and roughly $115 billion per annum of run-rate spending. Roughly 30,000 km of new ultra-high-voltage lines are scheduled, generating 300,000 tonnes per year of incremental copper demand at standard intensity assumptions of 40–60 t/km. Smart distribution networks add another 150,000 tonnes annually; EV charging infrastructure, at 60 kg of copper per pile and a 70 million-pile target by 2030, layers on roughly 4.2 million tonnes of cumulative demand over the period. Chinese grid spend gained 19.8% year-over-year through the first five months of 2025. Residential property starts in China have continued to bleed. Both statements are simultaneously true, and consensus analysis that aggregates them into "Chinese demand" hides a divergence that sophisticated buyers on the LME and SHFE have already priced. Beneath the grim property headlines, refined copper consumption in China in 2024 grew, not declined, and Chinese cathode imports remained at the upper end of historical ranges into Q1 2026. The third vector — defense and industrial onshoring — was treated as a footnote in the original paper. It deserves a section. Simon Hunt Strategic Services estimated military copper consumption at 2.186 million tonnes in 2021, roughly 9% of global refined output, and growing at approximately 14% per year through 2026. That is not a typo. The U.S. Department of Defense's plan to scale 155mm shell production from 93,000 to 1.2 million units annually by 2025 alone consumes a recognizable share of US smelter output. Each Virginia-class nuclear submarine — and the U.S. Navy is now building two per year alongside one Columbia-class ballistic missile boat under the so-called "1+2" cadence, the highest tonnage cadence since World War II — is a copper-intensive platform from hull to electronics to drives. AUKUS adds roughly eight nuclear submarines to Australia's order book in the 2030s. Japan is at 2% of GDP and rising; Korea is expanding shipyard and missile capacity; European NATO has cleared 3.5% of GDP in commitments. Wood Mackenzie's working estimate of 25,000–40,000 tonnes per annum of additional European-defense copper demand is, in our view, the floor; aggregating Asian rearmament, U.S. ammunition replenishment, and the AUKUS submarine pipeline pushes the sustained additional draw to 80,000–120,000 tonnes per annum over the next five years. Layered atop that is the IRA-and-CHIPS-Act-driven onshoring of semiconductor fabs, transformer manufacturing, and copper-intensive grid equipment — a structural shift in where copper is consumed, not whether it is consumed. None of this demand is interest-rate-sensitive. None of it cancels in a recession. Defense budgets are sticky on the way up and stickier on the way down once production lines are humming. The aggregate picture: the IEA's 2025 Critical Minerals Outlook projects copper demand growth of 2–3% annually through 2030, with the energy-transition share alone reaching 40% of total demand by decade-end. 4. THE GAP
Demand growth at 2.5% per year on a base of ~26 million tonnes implies roughly 650,000 tonnes of additional refined copper required each year. Mine supply growth in 2026 will be below 1%, per ICSG, against a project pipeline that has been hollowed out by capex restraint and permitting collapse. Secondary supply (recycling) tops out at roughly 9 million tonnes globally and grows at low single digits. Run the arithmetic forward five years: cumulative demand additions of roughly 3.5 million tonnes, against a pipeline that — even under optimistic assumptions about Quebrada Blanca, Oyu Tolgoi underground, Kamoa-Kakula recovery, and the handful of new African projects — adds perhaps 2.5 million tonnes net of depletion. 5. GEOPOLITICS
The single most important geopolitical event for copper in the past nine months is the Trump administration's Section 232 proclamation issued April 2, 2026, effective April 6, restructuring the tariff regime put in place by Proclamation 10962 of July 30, 2025. The original July 2025 proclamation had imposed a 50% tariff on semi-finished copper products and copper-intensive derivatives while exempting refined copper, with the recommended phased imposition of a 15% universal tariff on refined copper in 2027 rising to 30% in 2028 left as a future decision pending Commerce Department review by June 30, 2026. The April 2, 2026 proclamation did not roll back the regime. It tightened it, and it nuanced it.
The architecture is now five-tiered. Annex I-A — articles substantially made of copper, aluminum, or steel — pays 50% on full customs value. Annex I-B — derivative products — pays 25% on full customs value. Annex III — metal-intensive industrial and electrical grid equipment — pays a temporary blended 15% rate through December 31, 2027, after which it transitions to the 25% Annex I-B regime. The United Kingdom, under the bilateral framework, receives 25% on Annex I-A goods and 15% on Annex I-B goods provided the qualifying metal was smelted or cast in the UK. Critically, products manufactured abroad using copper that was smelted and cast in the United States receive a flat 10% rate regardless of annex. Russian aluminum derivatives remain at 200%. The "derivatives inclusions process" that had been open quarterly is terminated; future expansions occur only through joint USTR–Commerce action. Manufacturing drawback is permitted under 19 U.S.C. § 1313(a)–(b) for Annex I-B and Annex III articles from UK, EU, Japan, Korea, Mexico, Canada, or any future Agreement on Reciprocal Trade partner. The White HouseThe White House
The proclamation also introduces the so-called "15% clause": articles outside HTSUS chapters 72, 73, 74, and 76 in which the aggregate weight of steel, aluminum, and copper is less than 15% of total product weight are exempt entirely. This is the nuance that the consensus has not yet digested. The 15% clause materially shrinks the universe of products subject to full tariff exposure. Practically, it means a substantial fraction of the imported manufactured goods that previously generated forward-buying incentive — items where copper is present but constitutes a small share by weight — fall out of the regime entirely. That, in turn, reduces the structural incentive for the U.S. to "siphon" global LME tonnage into COMEX warehouses on speculative front-running. The economically trapped 590,000 tonnes that piled up in COMEX-registered warehouses by early 2026 — the highest level since 2003, exceeding the prior record by a wide margin — is now, on the margin, less compelling as a stockpile thesis than it was in summer 2025. 6. SUBSTITUTION RISK
This section is deliberately overweighted in this paper, because that is where consensus systematically underweights risk. Anyone who tells you copper "cannot be substituted" is selling something.
Aluminum is the proven substitute in many electrical applications. The aluminum-to-copper ratio at the conductor level matters: at sustained ratios of 4-to-1 or wider — meaning copper trading at four times aluminum's price — substitution accelerates measurably in overhead transmission, building wire, and certain motor applications. With LME copper at ~$13,000/t and LME aluminum at roughly $2,800–$3,100/t, the ratio sits at roughly 4.2–4.6x. We are inside the substitution-incentive zone. CRU and Wood Mackenzie estimate that sustained ratios above 4x for 18 months trigger a measurable uptick in aluminum substitution at the conductor level; over five years, this displaces 200,000–400,000 tonnes of incremental copper demand. That is real. It is not, however, large enough to close the deficit on its own.
Sodium-ion batteries are the wildcard. CATL, BYD, and HiNa have all advanced commercial sodium-ion chemistries with significant cost advantages in stationary storage and entry-level EVs. If sodium-ion captures 15% of the EV battery market by 2030, the marginal copper intensity per vehicle declines modestly (sodium-ion uses aluminum current collectors instead of copper). Bloomberg NEF's December 2025 Transition Metals Outlook estimates this could subtract 150,000–250,000 tonnes per year of forecast copper demand by 2030 — material, but again insufficient to flip the balance.
The third substitution risk is silver in conductivity-critical applications, which is structurally limited because silver is 80x more expensive per kilogram than copper and the supply-demand balance for silver is even tighter than copper's. The fourth is fiber optics displacing copper in data infrastructure, which is real at the long-haul level but has been more than offset by copper-intensive AI cooling and high-density power distribution at the rack level.
Aggregate substitution risk: 350,000–650,000 tonnes per year of demand displacement potential by 2030. Aggregate deficit: 200,000–500,000 tonnes per year on consensus forecasts, expanding thereafter. Substitution tightens the deficit. It does not close it. Anyone modeling this scenario as a bear case is double-counting demand.

8. EXPRESSING THE TRADE
Copper exposure for institutional capital can be expressed across a spectrum: physical futures (CME HG, LME 3M), structured notes embedded in cathode warrants, copper-mining equities (BHP, Rio Tinto, Freeport-McMoRan, Southern Copper, Antofagasta, Teck/post-merger Anglo Teck, Glencore, First Quantum, Ivanhoe), copper-junior equities (Solaris Resources, Hot Chili, Filo Mining, Capstone), royalty/streaming (Wheaton Precious Metals, Triple Flag, Sandstorm), and ETFs (CPER for futures, COPX for miners, GOEX for copper-gold producers).
For a 2-to-5 year horizon and an institutional risk budget, the cleanest expression is a barbell: 60–70% in diversified majors (BHP, Rio Tinto, Freeport-McMoRan, post-merger Anglo Teck) for liquidity and dividend yield; 20–30% in pure-play, single-asset accelerators (Ivanhoe, Antofagasta, First Quantum if Cobre Panama resolves) for torque; 5–10% in royalty/streaming for hedged exposure to mine-development upside without operational risk. We argue against straight CPER exposure because the contango structure that has prevailed in LME during periods of speculative excess has produced negative roll yield that has destroyed several percent of annualized return relative to spot. Where futures exposure is required, calendar spreads or physically-deliverable warrants on the LME are preferable. Freeport-McMoRan deserves a structural overweight: its 2027–2028 EBITDA guidance ($15.5 billion average) at $5/lb assumed copper, against a current price 13% above that assumption, against an enterprise value of roughly $89 billion, implies forward EV/EBITDA multiples of 7.5x for 2026 and 5.8x for 2027 — multiples that are simply incompatible with a structurally tight commodity unless one expects mean reversion that the supply data does not support.
8.1 Timing of Entry — A Sub-Section the Original Paper Owed Its Readers
The original paper recommended waiting for $10,000–$11,000/t LME as a "favorable entry." It owed the reader a serious examination of how probable that pullback is, how long one would wait, and what the opportunity cost of waiting actually is.
Historical base rates first. In copper bull markets since 1990 — measured as periods of sustained price appreciation lasting 18 months or longer — pullbacks of 15–20% from interim highs have occurred, on average, every 9–14 months. The 2003–2008 supercycle saw three such pullbacks (mid-2004, mid-2006, and the 2008 collapse, the last of which proved to be regime change rather than pullback). The 2009–2011 recovery saw two pullbacks in the 15–20% range. The 2020–2024 cycle saw pullbacks in mid-2021, summer 2022, and Q4 2023, each driven by distinct macro triggers: supply-chain reopening, China zero-COVID lockdown, and Fed hawkish repricing respectively. A 15–20% pullback from $13,000/t LME implies $10,400–$11,050/t. The base rate suggests such a pullback is more likely than not within a 12-to-18-month window. Probable, not guaranteed.
The macro triggers historically associated with such pullbacks are well-defined. China growth scares (2015–2016 yuan devaluation produced a 22% drawdown over four months); Fed pivots toward hawkishness (Q4 2018 trade-war episode produced an 18% drawdown in copper); recession fears (Q3 2022 produced a 27% drawdown); dollar strength episodes (mid-2014 produced a 14% drawdown). The 2020 COVID drawdown produced a 27% decline in six weeks before reversing into the largest copper bull market on record. In each of these episodes, the pullback was triggered by a macro event, was completed within four to eight weeks, and was followed by a higher subsequent high within twelve months.
The opportunity cost of waiting is non-trivial. From a $13,000/t entry, missing 100 basis points per month in the first six months while waiting for an entry that does not materialize equals 6% of forgone return — material against expected annualized returns of 10–15% in copper equities over the horizon. The carry on cash held aside to deploy is currently positive at roughly 4% on a US dollar basis, but that does not offset the risk of permanent foregone exposure if the structural deficit drives the next leg before any pullback materializes.
The practical entry strategy we recommend is staged scale-in rather than wait-for-pullback. Specifically: deploy 40% of intended copper allocation immediately at prevailing prices on the basis that the structural thesis does not require a perfect entry; reserve 40% for deployment on a confirmed 12% pullback from spot (a softer trigger than the historical 15–20%, calibrated to the elevated structural floor implied by the April 2 tariff regime); reserve the final 20% for a "stress event" deployment at a 20%+ pullback should one occur, recognizing this tranche may never deploy. This framework gets capital working, captures upside in the more probable continuation scenario, and retains optionality for the lower-probability deep-pullback scenario. It is the discipline of a trader, not the paralysis of an analyst waiting for the perfect chart.
9. SCENARIOS AND PROBABILITIES
The original paper assigned a 25/50/25 distribution to bull/base/bear scenarios. We have re-derived these from scratch and arrived at a different conclusion.
Bull case (LME $14,000–$18,000/t average over 2026–2030): 35% probability. Requires only one or two of: continued Grasberg under-recovery into 2027; further Codelco operating decline; Cobre Panama remaining shuttered through 2027; the U.S. proceeding with the 15%/30% phased refined-copper tariff in 2027–2028; State Grid executing on its $574 billion plan as guided; defense demand reaching the upper end of estimates; AI data center buildout exceeding Goldman's 420,000 tpa estimate. Each of these events has individual probability above 40%; the conjunction of any two has joint probability well above the aggregate bull-case threshold. The fact that consensus targets cluster around the bottom of this range ($11,000–$13,000/t) does not lower the probability that the world is bullish; it lowers the probability that the world is correctly priced.
Base case (LME $11,000–$14,000/t average): 40% probability. Roughly the consensus view: deficits realize but at the lower end of forecasts, supply recovers gradually from the Grasberg/El Teniente disruptions, China grid spend offsets property weakness without exceeding it, defense demand layers in modestly. Note: that consensus is already pricing this case; that lowers its true forward probability because reality has to match expectation. Markets do not pay you to be right about the consensus.
Bear case (LME below $10,000/t average): 25% probability. Requires the conjunction of: a synchronized U.S.–China–Europe recession reducing demand by 3%+ (individual probability over five years: ~30%); sustained Cu/Al ratio above 4.5x triggering accelerated substitution (individual: ~35%); sodium-ion EV penetration exceeding 15% by 2030 (individual: ~25%); a cluster of currently-stalled greenfield projects all delivering on time (individual: ~20%); a recycling supply surprise of 1+ million tonnes (individual: ~15%). The joint probability of three or more of these materializing simultaneously is, on naive independence assumptions, well under 10%; allowing for correlated negative shocks (recession would correlate with substitution incentive and weaker recycling) produces a realistic joint probability of 20–25%.
This is not a "default neutral" 25/50/25. It is a structural lean toward the long side that reflects the asymmetry of the underlying conjunctions. It is also more honest than a forecasting framework that splits the difference because the analyst is afraid to make a call.
10. WHAT I COULD BE WRONG ABOUT
The scenarios above could be wrong in several specific ways. China's grid investment could decelerate sharply if local government finances deteriorate further and Beijing chooses to reorient toward consumption rather than infrastructure — State Grid is state-owned, but state-owned does not mean budget-immune. The April 2 tariff regime could be diluted further or rolled back entirely under domestic political pressure as inflationary pass-through becomes politically costly; the Goldman Sachs view that "tariff announcements are unlikely in H1 2026 due to inflation pressure" reflects this risk. Sodium-ion battery commercialization could surprise to the upside more rapidly than BloombergNEF currently models, particularly if Chinese subsidies are redirected from copper-intensive lithium-ion to copper-light alternatives. The Grasberg recovery could outpace Freeport's H1/H2 cadence and add 100,000+ tonnes of unanticipated supply by mid-2027. Codelco could, under the incoming Kast administration, accelerate divestitures or partnerships that bring private-sector capital to El Teniente faster than the company's own glacial pace — bullish for production, bearish for tightness. AI data center buildout could slow if the macro environment tightens or if sovereign wealth funding pulls back from hyperscale projects.
The most underweighted risk in this paper is recycling. Secondary supply currently provides 9 million tonnes, or 35% of total. A 5% surprise in recycling — driven by sustained higher prices incentivizing more aggressive scrap collection, or by U.S. policy reorientation toward domestic scrap retention — adds 450,000 tonnes annually. That moves the needle materially.
11. INVALIDATORS
A disciplined trade requires explicit invalidators. The thesis defended in this paper is invalidated by the realization of any of the following, individually:
LME three-month copper trading sustainably below $9,500/t for more than three consecutive months without a corresponding supply-side shock to explain the move (would indicate demand destruction the thesis does not anticipate). The COMEX–LME spread inverting persistently to LME premium over COMEX (would indicate U.S. demand collapse). Treatment and refining charges recovering to $80/t or higher on a benchmark basis (would indicate concentrate availability has unexpectedly resolved). Aggregate top-20 miner copper capex rising more than 25% year-over-year (would indicate capital discipline has broken; supply will respond). Chinese refined copper imports falling more than 15% on a trailing twelve-month basis (would indicate the grid-investment thesis is failing to materialize in physical demand). The Trump administration formally rescinding Section 232 copper tariffs in their entirety (would remove the structural North American premium). Sodium-ion batteries achieving 20%+ EV market share by end-2028 (would compress the demand growth trajectory by more than substitution analysis currently models).
Each of these is observable in real time. Each is a falsification, not a forecast. If two or more print, the thesis should be reduced; if three or more print, the thesis should be exited.
CONCLUSION
The structural picture for copper on a 2-to-5 year horizon is bullish in a way that is not adequately reflected in consensus pricing, even after the substantial 2025 rally. Supply is constrained by a rare conjunction of geological, operational, capital-discipline, and geopolitical factors, none of which resolve quickly. Demand is buttressed by infrastructure already approved, by defense budgets already legislated, and by a Chinese grid program that decouples copper consumption from the property cycle that consensus narrative obsesses over. The April 2, 2026 tariff restructuring nuances rather than negates the policy backdrop, compresses the COMEX–LME premium, and embeds a 10% U.S.-smelted-copper preference that is a permanent margin advantage for domestically-integrated producers. Substitution risk is real, modeled, and insufficient to close the deficit. The base case consensus ($11,000–$13,000/t) is, in our view, the floor of a reasonable forward range, not its midpoint.
The trade is to deploy capital now in stages, to weight the diversified majors, to maintain torque through pure-play single-asset accelerators, and to watch the named invalidators rigorously. The honest answer to "what could go wrong" is "several specific things that we have named and can monitor." That is a trade. The alternative — waiting for the perfect entry that historical base rates suggest may not arrive on the manager's preferred schedule — is the discipline of an analyst who never has to answer for opportunity cost. We prefer the trade.
